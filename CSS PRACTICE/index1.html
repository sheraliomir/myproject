History of Radiohead

Radiohead are an English rock band formed in Abingdon,Oxfordshire, in 1985. The band consists of Thom Yorke (vocals, guitar, piano, keyboards), brothers Jonny Greenwood (lead guitar, keyboards, other instruments) and Colin Greenwood (bass), Ed O'Brien (guitar, backing vocals) and Philip Selway (drums, percussion). They have worked with producer Nigel Godrich and cover artist Stanley Donwood since 1994. Radiohead's experimental approach is credited with advancing the sound of alternative rock. mmm

Style of songwriting

Radiohead's musical style has been described as art rock,[30] alternative rock,[235] electronica,[238] experimental rock,[241] progressive rock,[232] Britpop,[232] grunge,[232] art pop,[242] and electronic rock.[243] Joe Taysom of Far Out referred to Radiohead technically as a stadium rock band.[244] Among Radiohead's earliest influences were Queen,[245] Bob Dylan,[245] Pink Floyd and Elvis Costello, post-punk acts such as Joy Division,[245] Siouxsie and the Banshees[245][246] and Magazine, and significantly 1980s alternative rock bands such as R.E.M.,[245] U2, the Pixies, the Smiths and Sonic Youth.[247] Jonny Greenwood named Magazine guitarist John McGeoch his biggest guitar influence.[248] By the mid-1990s, Radiohead began to adopt recording methods from hip hop, inspired by the sampling work of DJ Shadow,[2] and became interested in using computers to generate sounds.[249] Other influences include the soundtracks of Ennio Morricone, 1960s rock groups such as the Beatles and the Beach Boys, and Phil Spector's "wall of sound" production.[2][31] Radiohead have cited 60s and 70s jazz artists such as Miles Davis, Charles Mingus and Alicer Coltrane as influences.[250] According to Jonny Greenwood, "We bring in our favourite jazz albums, and say: we want to do this. And we enjoy the sound of our failing!"[250] He likened their jazz influence to 1950s English bands imitating American blues records.[250] Drummer Clive Deamer, who has recorded and performed with Radiohead since 2011, said Radiohead do not see themselves as a rock band and that their methodology had closer parallels with jazz: "They deliberately try to avoid clich√© and standard forms for the sake of the song ... Rock bands don't do that. It's far more like a jazz mentality."
